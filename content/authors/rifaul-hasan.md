# About Rifaul Hasan

Rifaul Hasan has spent 7 years working with students in residential school settings. He understands the unique pressures that come with living away from home at a young age, dealing with strict routines, and competing with peers in both academics and sports.

He joined Sainik School Guide India because he noticed that most online content about Sainik Schools focused on admissions and exams, but almost nobody talked about what happens after a student gets in. The adjustment period, homesickness, peer pressure, and academic stress are real issues that families need to prepare for.

## Background

Rifaul is from Guwahati, Assam. He completed his master's in psychology from Gauhati University and his counseling certification from the Indian Institute of Counseling. He started his career as a school counselor at a residential school in Assam, where he worked with students aged 11 to 18.

Over the years, he has counseled more than 300 students dealing with various challenges: homesickness, academic pressure, relationship issues with peers, body image concerns, and career confusion. His approach is practical rather than clinical. He believes in listening first, then helping students find their own solutions.

## What He Covers

**Student Mental Health** - Articles about managing stress, dealing with homesickness, building confidence, and developing resilience. He writes about real situations students face, not textbook scenarios.

**Parent-Student Communication** - How parents can stay connected with their children in residential schools without being overbearing. When to step in and when to let the child handle things independently.

**Academic Pressure** - Managing expectations, dealing with competition, recovering from poor exam performance, and building sustainable study habits.

**Personal Development** - Building leadership skills, handling peer pressure, developing discipline without losing individuality, and preparing for life after school.

## Writing Approach

Rifaul writes like he's talking to a student or parent directly. His articles are conversational and empathetic, but not soft. He gives honest advice, even when it's uncomfortable. If a parent is putting too much pressure on their child, he says so. If a student is avoiding a real problem, he addresses it.

He uses examples from his counseling experience (with names and details changed for privacy) because he knows that real stories resonate more than abstract advice.
